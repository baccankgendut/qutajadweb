Early Life & Biography

Popularly known as Governor Moonbeam, Jerry Brown was not inclined toward politics in his early years. In 1958 he became a Jesuit as he wanted to become a priest. It was the same year that his father Pat Brown was elected Governor. But he soon found that priesthood was not his calling and in 1960 gave it up to pursue law. He graduated from Yale Law School in 1964. Post-graduation, he practiced law and worked for well-known law firms. However, it was the humiliating defeat of Pat Brown in the election of 1966 to Ronald Reagan that made Brown Jr determined to re-establish his family name in the world of politics.

Career

It was in 1970 that he entered public life when he ran successfully for Secretary of State. But what he sought was revenge from Reagan for defeating his father and finally in 1976 after considerable groundwork. But the election did not go as planned, and he barely won the seat. As Governor, Brown’s actions and decisions were rather controversial, like siding with the unpopular labor activist Cesar Chavez and appointing his personal friend Rose Bird, as Chief Justice of the state Supreme Court, despite her inexperience.

In 1978 he had a landslide victory with over 1.3 million votes, breaking the record his father set in 1958. Unfortunately, despite his conscious efforts to keep Reagan at bay, his 1978 victory was short-lived, and in 1980, Ronald Reagan was elected President. He made another desperate attempt in 1982 and ran for the U.S. Senate but lost it. Brown kept away from the political scene for some time returning only in 1989 to become California’s state Democratic Chairman. Though his popularity had waned, he campaigned for President in 1992. Of course, he lost that election but brought to the fore the Whitewater Scandal that finally spelled doom for Bill Clinton.

In the interim, he returned to practicing law before being elected as Mayor of Oakland in the San Fransisco bay area. He changed his strategy and focused on fighting crime, bringing business downtown, and encouraging charter schools, which later helped him win the reelection in 2002. But he is remembered for his historic comeback in 2006when he ran for state Attorney General, winning it by a wide margin. He finally took retirement in January 2019 after five decades in politics.
